A*

Parameters

  • $f$ is the estimate of the total cost
    • At each step, A* picks the node having the lowest $f = h + g$.

  • $g$ is the cost to move from the starting point to a node under consideration, following the path generated to get there.

  • $h$ is the estimated cost to move from that node to the final destination.
    • This is referred to as the heuristic, which is essentially a smart guess. The actual distance to the goal cannot be determined with certainty until the goal has been reached because all sorts of obstructions could be in the way. There can be many ways to calculate $h$.
The cost $f$ for different plans from the first starting point would be:
  • Plan 1 = [start, x9] = 6
  • Plan 2 = [start, x15] = 6
  • Plan 3 = [start, x23] = 6
  • Plan 4 = [start, x17] = 4

Properties of the heuristic

  • Admissable
    • It needs to be an underestimate of the true cost. One way to do this is to ignore the cost of going around obstacles. Overestimating the true cost would lead a robot to not expand plans as much to find the lowest cost plan possible.

  • Consistent

    • A heuristic function has to obey the triangle inequality theorem, which states that for any three points, x1, x2, and x3, the heuristic estimate for x1 to x3 has to be less than the heuristic estimate from x1 to x2 plus the heuristic estimate from x2 to x3. $$ H[x_1, x_3] \leq H[x_1, x_2] + H[x_2, x_3] $$

Advantages

  • It is guaranteed to find the lowest cost plan first before any others.

  • It doesn't suffer the inefficiencies of breadth-first search, where the list of partial plans can get unwieldy.

    • This is dependent on the quality of the heuristic - the better the heuristic, the better the A-star implementation will perform.
