ADC

Working

An ADC measures an analog voltage (like from a sensor) and converts it into a digital number. To do this, it first samples the voltage by charging a tiny internal capacitor to the same level as the input voltage.

Sample time

Sample time is the amount of time the ADC connects an internal capacitor to an input pin to allow it to charge.

The sample time can be set to 480 ADC clock cycles for example by setting the required configuration in a register.

The sample time is chosen based on the impedance (resistance) of the analog source.
  • For a low impedance (strong signal) source, a short sample time is fine (eg: 3 cycles, this is fast!)
  • A high impedance (weak sensor, a potentiometer) source cannot "push" a lot of current. The ADC's internal capacitor needs more time to charge up to the correct voltage. Using a long sample time like 480 cycles ensures an accurate measurement, even though it makes the conversion slower.

Clock

Considering an STM32F4 microcontroller with a HCLK of 180MHz. $$ \text{ADCCLK} = \frac{\text{PCLK2}}{\text{ADC_Prescaler}} $$ PCLK2 is the clock frequency of APB2 which is usually 90MHz.

To achieve a 22.5MHz ADC clock frequency, the prescaler would have to be $4$.
