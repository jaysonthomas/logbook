Terminology

Setting refers to the initial stage in which a material undergoes a transformation from a wet or pliable state to a solid or rigid state. This process often involves physical or chemical changes that enable the material to become hard enough to be worked with or shaped.

Curing refers to the subsequent process during which a material continues to develop its properties and achieve its ultimate strength, durability, and other desired characteristics. Curing occurs after the material has set.

Drying primarily involves the removal of moisture or solvents, whereas setting involves chemical or physical changes that result in the hardening of the material.

When to sand

Sanding a filler too early can result in:

  • Incomplete filling
    • Sanding a filler while it's dried but not set may remove some of the material prematurely which could result in an uneven or incomplete filling of the gaps.

  • Poor adhesion
    • Sanding too early can disturb the bonding of the filler to the substrate. Filler materials are designed to adhere to the surface and form a solid bond. Sanding too soon can weaken this bond and reduce the effectiveness of the repair.

To achieve the best results, it's generally advisable to wait until the filler has fully set but has not yet fully cured before sanding.
