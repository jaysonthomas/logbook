Definitions: Variance

Variance

[Stackexchange question. Code example.]

When drawing from a distribution repeatedly, variance gives an indication of how spread out the samples (the values being drawn) are from the mean. Variance is not a measure of how well the mean is known, but how much the samples vary. $$ \sigma^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{N}(x_i-\mu)^2P(X=x_i) $$ $\sigma$ is the standard deviation which is the square root of variance.

When the probability of every outcome $P(x_i)$ is $\frac{1}{N}$, the variance is just the average of the squared differences from the mean. $$ \sigma^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{N}\frac{(x_i-\mu)^2}{N} $$

Equation deepdive

Subtracting the mean makes the mean the zero point. It is a shift operation. All sample values can now be represented w.r.t the mean as simple deviations as shown here. Squaring the deviations as opposed to taking the raw or absolute value, helps to amplify the deviations which is helpful when comparing 2 different distributions.

Sample variance

Assuming the probability of every observation is the same, in calculating sample variance, $N-1$ is used in the division instead of $N$: $$ \sigma^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{N}\frac{(x_i-\mu)^2}{N-1} $$ Dividing by $N - 1$ adjusts for the fact that the sample mean is itself an estimate based on the data, and is not known with certainty. This adjustment reduces the bias in the estimate of the population variance, making it an unbiased estimator of the population variance.

When dividing by $N$, the resulting estimate is biased low, meaning it tends to underestimate the population variance. This bias can be substantial for small samples, but becomes negligible as the sample size increases.

Standard deviation

It controls the spread or width of the values of a distribution around its mean. Smaller $\sigma$ means less variability and a narrower peak.

In a normal distribution:

  • ~68% of values fall within $\pm 1$ standard deviation of the mean.
  • ~95% fall within $\pm 2 \sigma$.
  • ~99.7% fall within $\pm 3 \sigma$.
