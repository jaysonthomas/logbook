Basis vectors

Overview

The red and green arrows in the following image are the basis vectors of the coordinate system (the grid in the background).
Basis vectors are fixed to a coordinate system. If the latter is stretched or rotated (transformed), the basis vectors also end up being transformed. From here on, the coordinate system will not be drawn. Any transformation of the basis vectors implies a transformation to its corresponding coordinate system.

Basis vectors are like a ruler for a coordinate system. As long as the ruler isn't zero and isn't pointing in the same direction as another ruler in the set, it can be used to measure and describe any point in the space. Depending on the ruler's tick marks, it will lead to different coordinates (coefficients) for the same point.

  • A unit vector is like a ruler where the tick marks are exactly 1 unit apart.
  • A non-unit vector is like a ruler where the tick marks are 5 units apart or 0.5 units apart.

Criteria

To be a basis for a space, a set of vectors needs to satisfy two specific conditions:
  • Linear Independence: No vector in the set can be built by combining the others.
  • Spanning the Space: You can reach any point in the space by scaling and adding these vectors.

Types

  • Orthogonal: Unit vectors: No, Perpendicular: Yes
  • Orthonormal: Unit vectors: Yes, Perpendicular: Yes
If a basis is not normalised (unit length), the math just gets a bit "messier," but the underlying geometry remains valid. For example, calculations like the dot product or finding projections would involve dividing by the magnitude of the vectors every time if not normalised.
