Definiteness

Overview

Definiteness is the matrix version of a number's sign ($+$ or $-$).

In higher-dimensional math, the quadratic form ($x^\top A x$) is the equivalent of $x^2$.

  • Positive Definite: No matter the vector $x$, the result is always $> 0$.
  • Negative Definite: No matter the vector $x$, the result is always $< 0$.
  • Indefinite: Depending on $x$, the result could be positive or negative.
"Definite" means the matrix has a predictable, one-sided behavior. It either always scales things positively or always scales them negatively, never switching between the two.

Effect on shape

Definiteness of an underlying matrix determines the shape of a graph's surface.
  • Positive Definite: Geometric shape: A bowl facing up. Optimisation meaning: The origin is a global minimum.
  • Negative Definite: Geometric shape: A bowl facing down. Optimisation meaning: The origin is a global maximum.
  • Indefinite: Geometric shape: A saddle. Optimisation meaning: The origin is a saddle point (neither min or max).

Optimisation use case

To find the bottom of a cost function, the Hessian matrix (a matrix of second derivatives) is used.
  • If that matrix is Positive Definite, the bottom is the bottom of a valley (a minimum).
  • If it's Negative Definite, the bottom is the peak of a mountain (a maximum).

Characteristics of a negative definite matrix

  • Symmetry: $A = A^\top$.
  • Eigenvalues: All eigenvalues are strictly negative ($\lambda < 0$).
  • Determinants: The leading principal minors alternate in sign (starting with negative).
