Differentiation

Chain rule

To differentiate $y=f(g(x))$, let $u = g(x)$. Then $y = f(u)$ and $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \times \frac{du}{dx} $$

Example

$r = cost^2$

$r$ is a function of $\theta$ and $\theta$ is a function of $t$. We, therefore, have $r(\theta(t))$. $\theta$ is similar to $u$ and $t^2$ is similar to $g(x)$.

$r = cos\theta$ $$ \frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{dr}{d\theta} \times \frac{d\theta}{dt} $$

Miscellaneous

  • $\frac{d(L_1cos\theta_1)}{d\theta}$

    Differentiation of $sin$ keeps the sign the same.
