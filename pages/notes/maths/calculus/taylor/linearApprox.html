Linear approximation

Overview

This is also called a first-order Taylor expansion.

Intuition

Consider the derivative as the slope of a hill. To know how much the elevation changes as one moves forward, the steepness needs to be multiplied by the distance covered. $$ f(x + \Delta{x}) \approx f(x) + \frac{\partial{f}}{\partial{x}}\bigg|_x \, \Delta{x} $$
$$\begin{array}{ccl} f(x) &:& \text{Current location, starting height} \\ \frac{\partial{f}}{\partial{x}}\bigg|_x &:& \text{Steepness of the slope at the current spot. Rate of change.} \\ \Delta{x} &:& \text{Distance moved from the current spot.} \end{array}$$
If the slope is 2 (rising 2 meters for every 1 meter forward), for a forward movement of 0.5 meters, the height increases by $2 \times 0.5 = 1$ meter.

Derivation

The derivative is defined as the limit of the average rate of change: $$ \frac{df}{dx} = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x} $$ The derivative is treated as a fixed value for a very small $\Delta x$.

Rearranging:

$$\begin{align*} \frac{df}{dx} \cdot \Delta x &\approx f(x + \Delta x) - f(x) \\ f(x + \Delta x) &\approx f(x) + \frac{df}{dx} \Delta x \end{align*}$$ The term $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \Delta x$ represents the change in the function's value ($\Delta y$) predicted by the tangent line.
