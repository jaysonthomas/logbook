Notation

Gradient

Given $f(x)$: $\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ (an objective function that takes in a vector and outputs a scalar, for example): $$ \frac{\partial{f}}{\partial{x}} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times n} \quad \text{is a row vector} $$

$\frac{\partial{f}}{\partial{x}}$ is the gradient, a linear operator that maps $\Delta{x}$ into $\Delta{f}$.

Similarly, given $g(y)$: $\mathbb{R}^m \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n$: $$ \frac{\partial{g}}{\partial{y}} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m} $$ The dimension of the output determines the number of rows that the gradient has.

Reason

The notation-convention makes the chain rule work: $$ \underbrace{\frac{\partial{f}}{\partial{x}} \bigg|_{g(y)}}_{1 \times n} \, \underbrace{\frac{\partial{g}}{\partial{y}} \bigg|_{y}}_{n \times m} $$ Taylor expansion also works out dimensionally: $$ \underbrace{f(x)}_{1 \times 1} + \underbrace{\frac{\partial{f}}{\partial{x}}}_{1 \times n} \, \underbrace{\Delta{x}}_{n \times 1} $$

Chain rule occurrence

Taking the taylor series expansion on $f$: $$\begin{equation} f(x + \Delta{x}) \approx f(x) + \frac{\partial{f}}{\partial{x}} \, \Delta{x} \label{eq:1} \end{equation}$$ Consider that $x = g(y)$, then $$\begin{equation} \Delta{x} = \frac{\partial{g}}{\partial{y}} \, \Delta{y} \label{eq:2} \end{equation}$$ Taking the Taylor series expansion on $g(y)$: $$ g(y + \Delta{y}) \approx g(y) + \frac{\partial{g}}{\partial{y}} \, \Delta{y} $$ Taking this as the input to $f$ and substituting \ref{eq:2}: $$\begin{align*} f(g(y + \Delta{y})) &\approx f(g(y) + \frac{\partial{g}}{\partial{y}} \, \Delta{y}) \\ &\approx f(x + \Delta{x}) \end{align*}$$ \ref{eq:1} becomes: $$\begin{align*} f(g(y + \Delta{y})) &\approx f(x + \Delta{x}) \\ &\approx f(x) + \frac{\partial{f}}{\partial{x}} \, \Delta{x} \\ &\approx f(g(y)) + \frac{\partial{f}}{\partial{x}} \bigg|_{g(y)} \frac{\partial{g}}{\partial{y}} \bigg|_{y} \, \Delta{y} \end{align*}$$ [Partial derivative reference]
