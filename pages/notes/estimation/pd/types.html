Overview

[Ref]

When the outcomes are discrete, the probability distribution function is called a probability mass function. In the continuous space, it is called a probability density function. CDF is common to both.

Probability Mass function

A probability distribution divides/distributes the whole probability mass (equal to 1) of a random variable across its possible outcomes, the variable's sample space.

Mass

Mass is:
  • The quantity of matter in a physical body
  • A measure of an object's inertia, its resistance to acceleration when a net force is applied
Miscellaneous example uses: a mass of cyclists, the film has mass appeal.

Cumulative distribution function

[Further reading] $$ F_x(u) = p(x \leq u) $$ The function describes the probability that a random variable $x$ is less than or equal to $u$.

$F$ will be monotonically increasing. The limit of $F$ as $u$ goes to:

  • $-\infty$ is $0$
  • $+\infty$ is $1$
    • That is, with increasing values of $u$, more values of $x$ become less than $u$.

The index to the function is $u$, but it is sub-scripted by $x$ to indicate which random variable is being characterised by the probability that the value of that random variable is less than $u$ or not.

Probability density function

[Ref: Why it's okay for the probability density function to be greater than 1] $$ f_x(u) = \frac{d}{du}F_x(u) $$ It's the derivative of a CDF w.r.t. its index. $$ \text{Probability density} = \text{Slope of CDF} = \frac{\text{Probability of interval}}{\text{Interval length}} $$

From the image, the vehicle has a probabiliy density of $0.5$ for $0 \leq x \geq 2$, and $0$ everywhere else. The probability that the vehicle is in the region between 0 and 2 is found by calculating the area under the curve which would be $2*0.5$.

The probability that a random variable will take a value between $x_1$ and $x_2$ is given by the integral of the probability density function from $x_1$ to $x_2$: $$ p(x_1 < x < x_2) = \int^{x_2}_{x_1}f_x(u)dx = \int^{x_2}_{x_1}p(x)dx $$

$f_x(u)$ isn't the probability, and it can be greater than $1$. This density function is often written as $p(x)$. To be a proper density, $f_x(u)$ has to be greater than or equal to $0$, and the integral under the distribution has to be equal to $1$. Normalization implies finding a constant multiplier in front of this density function to make it integrate to $1$.
