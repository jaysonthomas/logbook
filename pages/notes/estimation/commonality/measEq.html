Measurement model

A measurement model encapsulated in the Jacobian $H$:
  • Projects a state into its measurement space or
  • Maps a state to a measurement
In the simplest form, if a measurement equation is linear, it will look like: $$ y = Hx + v $$ $y$ represents observations or measurements made.

The Jacobian

$H$, the Jacobian matrix, contains partial derivatives of the measurements with respect to the unknown parameter/state(s) $x$.

$H$ is easy to compute in the linear case but requires more mathematical effort in the case of non-linear estimation.

Fundamental principle

More the measurements, the more accurate the estimate will be.

Only the shape of the distribution w.r.t. the unknown $x$ is known. If the error $v$ is known, $x$ can be found: $Hx = y - v$. Or if $x$ is known, $v$ can be solved. Since neither is known, it becomes an estimation problem. A maximum likelihood estimation approach is used which, intuitively looks at the measurements and asks what state/parameter value is the most likely to yield the particular set of measurements.

An estimation problem boils down to figuring out the measurement model $H$ which is often the tricky bit.
