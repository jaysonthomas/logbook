Scipy: Multivariate normal

Overview

A 2D multivariate normal distribution is not calculated by finding two separate PDF values and combining them. It's a single joint probability distribution that describes the system as a whole - one value is calculated directly from the multivariate normal PDF formula, which inherently accounts for the relationship between the two variables.
  • Positive Covariance: When variable X is higher than its mean, variable Y also tends to be higher than its mean. The elliptical contour plot will be tilted diagonally upward.

  • Negative Covariance: When variable X is higher than its mean, variable Y tends to be lower than its mean. The ellipse will be tilted downward.

  • Zero Covariance: The variables are uncorrelated. There is no linear relationship between them. The ellipse will be aligned with the x and y axes.
The single formula incorporates:
  • The mean of both variables.
  • The variance (or standard deviation) of each variable.
  • The covariance between the variables.
The result is a single number representing the probability density on a 2D, bell-shaped surface which is often shaped like an ellipse when viewed from above.

If the variables are uncorrelated, the 2D PDF is simply the product of the two individual 1D PDFs: $$ P(x, y) = P(x) * P(y) \quad \text{(only when covariance is zero)} $$

Define the properties of the distribution

multivariate_normal(...) creates a distribution object with its properties:
  • The mean vector defines the center of the distribution where the peak lies.
  • The covariance matrix defines the shape, size, and orientation of the hill.
    • Variance (diagonal elements) describes how spread out or wide the hill is along each axis.
    • Covariance (off-diagonal elements) describes the relationship between the variables. If they are non-zero, the hill is tilted, resulting in an elliptical shape rather than a perfect circle.

stateSpace = multivariate_normal(mu, sigma)
stateSpace.pdf(mu)
.pdf computes the pdf value for a point specified as the argument. It already knows the mean and covariance when the object was created.
