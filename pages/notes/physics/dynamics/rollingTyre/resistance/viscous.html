Viscous friction

Overview

Viscous friction occurs whenever an object moves through a fluid (liquid or gas). In a car, this is primarily the shearing of oil layers inside the engine, transmission, and wheel bearings. It is speed dependent.
  • Static friction is highest when stationary
  • Viscous friction is 0 when stationary and increases with speed.
In a car's bearings, layers of oil separate the moving metal parts. As the axle spins, it has to drag these layers of oil past each other. The faster the axle spins, the more resistance the oil offers.

Air resistance is also a form of viscous friction, acting on the outside of a car.

Analogy

Consider the stirring of a honey pot. Static Friction is equivalent to getting a spoon moving which requires a tiny "pop" of force to break the surface tension or inertia.

Once moving, it is easier to stir slowly. With increasing speed, the honey resists more aggressively. This is similar to viscous friction.
