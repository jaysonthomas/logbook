Direction

Overview

Longitudinal friction force does not strictly follow the direction of motion. Instead, it acts in the direction opposite to the slip (or potential slip) of the tyre's contact patch. Depending on whether you are speeding up or slowing down, this friction can act either with or against the vehicle's motion.

Acceleration: Friction acts with motion

When speeding up, torque is applied to the wheels:
  • The Mechanism: The wheel tries to spin faster than the car is moving. This means the tyre's contact patch pushes backward against the pavement.
  • The Reaction: By Newton's Third Law, the pavement pushes forward on the tyre.
  • Result: The longitudinal friction force points in the same direction as the vehicle's velocity, propelling it forward.

Braking: Friction acts against motion

When slowing down, potentially a wheel is clamped:
  • The Mechanism: The wheel tries to spin slower than the car is moving (or lock up entyrely). The contact patch tries to slide forward across the pavement.
  • The Reaction: The pavement pushes backward on the tyre to resist this sliding.
  • Result: The longitudinal friction force points in the opposite direction of the vehicle's velocity, slowing it down.

Acceleration

When a tyre pushes against a road surface:
  • If static friction (due to the grip of the contact patch on the road) is not overcome, the tyre does not slip (loss of grip). This allows a reaction force, traction force, to push the tyre forward - it is the effective force driving the tyre forward.

  • Traction force acts in the direction of motion.

  • If a tyre begins to slip, the friction involved becomes kinetic friction, which acts opposite to the direction of sliding motion with a lower maximum value than static friction. The car then loses effective traction.

Deceleration

When braking, the aim is to allow a tyre's contact patch with the road to have a longer period gripping onto each other. This allows the tyre to slow down and eventually come to a halt. Traction force is still in the direction of motion.
