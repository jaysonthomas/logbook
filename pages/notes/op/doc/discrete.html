Discrete time

Overview

Discrete samples replace continuous time. $$ \min_{\substack{x_{1:N} \\ u_{1:N-1}}} \quad J(x_{1:N}, u_{1:N-1}) = \sum^{N-1}_{k=1} l(x_k, u_k) + l_f(x_N) \\ $$
$$\begin{array}{ccl} u_{1:N-1} &:& \text{No control action is taken at the terminal state. } \\ && \text{Hence, one less control action than states.} \\ \end{array}$$
Samples $x_k, u_k$ are often called knot points since often splines are used to interpolate between points. Often the optimisation is solved for course knot points (say 5Hz), but then upscaled (500Hz) by interpolating using cubic splines between the solutions which leads to very smooth outputs. Constraints are usually evaluated for the knot points and not for the finer interpolated points due to the higher computational expense.

Constrained optimisation problem

Constraints are enforced at each time step.

Dynamics constraints - RK4 could be used to obtain the below for example. $$ s.t. \quad x_{k+1} = f(x_k, u_k) $$ Torque limits $$ u_{min} \leq u_k \leq u_{max} \quad \forall k $$ Obstacle/safety constraints. For e.g. distance from the minimum radius around an object needs to be above some threshold. $$ c(x_k) \geq 0 \quad \forall k $$
