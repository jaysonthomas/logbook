Armijo condition

Overview

$$ \|r_n\|_2 > \|r\|_2 + b \; \alpha \; \frac{r}{\|r\|_2} \; J \; \Delta z $$ The residual at the current step $r$ is the objective function evaluated at the current step since the reference is 0.

The Armijo condition compares scalars (the new norm vs. the old norm). $J \Delta z$ is a vector which gives the predicted change in the residual vector. To turn this vector change into a scalar change in length, it needs to be projected onto the current residual vector.

In the first iteration, $r_n$ is based on the point $z + \Delta z$. In the remaining iterations, $r_n$ is based on $z + \alpha \; \Delta z$.

Examples

Moving Toward the Origin

Here, a step is perfectly aligned with reducing the error.

Consider that the residual, $r = [10, 0]$

$\|r\|_2 = \sqrt{10^2 + 0^2} = 10$
Unit Vector $\frac{r}{\|r\|} = [1, 0]$

Change: $J\Delta z = [-1, 0]$. A decrease of 1 unit in the first component is predicted.

$$ \frac{r}{\|r\|_2} \; J \; \Delta z = [1, 0] \cdot [-1, 0] = (1 \times -1) + (0 \times 0) = -1 $$ The Armijo condition sees a change of $-1$, a movement in the descent direction, so it expects a significant decrease in the norm.

Moving "Sideways" (Orthogonal)

Here, the step changes the residual vector, but not in a way that immediately makes it shorter.

Consider that the residual, $r = [10, 0]$

Change: $J\Delta z = [0, -1]$ (Predicting a change in the second component only) $$ \frac{r}{\|r\|_2} \; J \; \Delta z = [1, 0] \cdot [0, -1] = (1 \times 0) + (0 \times -1) = 0 $$ Even though the Jacobian says something is changing ($M\Delta z$ isn't zero), the norm isn't expected to change.
