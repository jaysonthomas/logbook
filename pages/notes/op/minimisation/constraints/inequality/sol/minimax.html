Minimax method

Reason for existence

Consider an objective function that is strongly convex, a bowl. Even if a solution starts of in an interior region in a constrained problem and converges to the minimum close to the constraint, it is possible for the eigen values of the Hessian to not be positive definite.

Since the objective function is convex, it is not the cause. The constraints cause it.

Setup

$$\begin{align*} \min_x \quad & f(x) \\ s.t. \quad & c(x) \geq 0 \end{align*}$$

Best case solution

$$\begin{align*} & \min_x \quad f(x) + \rho^-_\infty(c(x)) \\\\ & \rho^-_\infty (x) = \begin{cases} 0 & \quad c(x) \geq 0 \\ +\infty & \quad c(x) < 0 \end{cases} \end{align*}$$

Minimax method

A new lagrange multiplier term is introduced. This method is exactly equivalent to the impractical theoretical method. This method is closely related to the primal-dual trick and lagrange multipliers. $$ \min_x \quad \max_{\lambda \geq 0} \quad f(x) - \lambda^\top c(x) \\ $$ Similar to the KKT conditions, $\lambda$ has to be positive which turns the maximisation into a constrained maximisation problem.

When $c(x) < 0$, the inner problem blows up.

When solving a primal-dual problem involving optimising over $x$ and $\lambda$, the optimiser is trying to find a saddle point. When minimising w.r.t. x, the function w.r.t. x needs to be convex locally, a right-side-up bowl. When maximising w.r.t. $\lambda$, the function w.r.t. $\lambda$ is an upside-down bowl.

Regularisation

KKT conditions define a saddle point in ($x, \lambda$). KKT system should have dim(x) positive eigenvalues and dim($\lambda$) negative eigenvalues at an optimum. This is called being quasi-definite. $$ \begin{bmatrix} H + \beta I & c^\top \\ c & -\beta I \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \Delta x \\ \Delta \lambda \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -\nabla_x L \\ -c(x) \end{bmatrix} \quad \beta > 0 $$ A positive regulariser is used on $x$ and a negative one on $\lambda$ to force the right kind of saddle - it forces gradient descent on the x's and ascent on the $\lambda$s.

A matrix is quasi-definite if it can be partitioned into a positive definite block and a negative definite block.

Intuition

When $\beta I$ and $-\beta I$ are added to the Hessian matrix, they essentially bend the geometry of the optimization landscape to ensure the solver behaves predictably.
  • Positive Regularizer ($H + \beta I$):By adding a positive value to the diagonal of the primal block, we ensure that the eigenvalues associated with $x$ are shifted in the positive direction. This makes the local surface look more like a "valley" (convex). In this geometry, the natural direction for the solver is downward toward the bottom of the valley.

  • Negative Regularizer ($-\beta I$):By subtracting a positive value (adding a negative one) from the dual block, we force the eigenvalues associated with $\lambda$ to be negative. This makes the surface look like a "peak" (concave) in the $\lambda$ dimension. In this geometry, the natural direction for the solver is upward toward the top of the peak.

$\beta$ terms act as "steering." Without them, if the Hessian $H$ is singular or "flat," the solver might get lost. By forcing the $x$-block to be positive and the $\lambda$-block to be negative, you mathematically guarantee that the algorithm treats $x$ as something to be minimized and $\lambda$ as something to be maximized, effectively "locking in" the saddle point search.

Saddle point

Around the saddle point, the terrain is positive definite in the x directions and negative definite in the $\lambda$ directions.

The Curvature (Definiteness)

This describes the "bend" of the surface.
  • $x$ directions: The Hessian with respect to $x$ ($\nabla_{xx}^2 L$) should be Positive Definite. This creates the "right-side-up bowl" that allows for a minimum.

  • $\lambda$ directions: The Hessian with respect to $\lambda$ ($\nabla_{\lambda\lambda}^2 L$) is actually zero in a linear constraint problem (since $\lambda$ is usually linear in the Lagrangian), but in a general sense, the "Dual" objective is Concave. Concavity implies Negative Semi-Definite.

The Domain (Constraints)

This is just the allowed range. Saying $\lambda \geq 0$ is like saying "You can only walk on the right side of the street." It doesn't change the fact that the street might be sloped like an upside-down bowl. Imagine a mountain ridge (the $\lambda$ direction) that is cut off by a wall at $\lambda = 0$.
  • If the "peak" of the mountain is at $\lambda = 5$, the optimizer stops at 5.
  • If the "peak" of the mountain is actually at $\lambda = -2$, the optimizer wants to go there but hits the wall at $\lambda = 0$.
In the second case, the "saddle point" happens right against the boundary. This is exactly what happens in the KKT conditions:
  • If the constraint is inactive, $\lambda = 0$ (the optimizer is pushed against the wall).
  • If the constraint is active, $\lambda > 0$ (the optimizer found a peak away from the wall).

Intuition

If the solution is at a point that is a minimum for $x$ but not a maximum for $\lambda$, the dual player will move $\lambda$ to increase the value. If the solution is at a maximum for $\lambda$ but not a minimum for $x$, the primal player will move $x$ to decrease it. The only point where neither player wants to move is the saddle point.

Visualisation

Imagine a physical horse saddle.
  • If one moves front-to-back (along the horse's spine), one would be at the bottom of a curve. This represents the $x$ dimension (minimisation).
  • If one moves side-to-side (where the legs hang), one would be at the peak of a curve. This represents the $\lambda$ dimension (maximization).
At the very center of the saddle, one is at a minimum relative to one direction and a maximum relative to the other.
