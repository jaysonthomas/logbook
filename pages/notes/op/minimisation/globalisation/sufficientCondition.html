Sufficient condition

Overview

The interest is in the min case alone. At a minimum, second order sufficient conditions (a condition on the Hessian of the objective function) dictate that the curve needs to be both flat and curved up around it.

Scalar case

Considering a Newton step, in the scalar case, the division will only consist of scalars, i.e. the Jacobian and Hessian are scalars. $$ \Delta{x} = - \left(\nabla^2 f(x)\right)^{-1} \nabla{f(x)} $$
$$\begin{array}{ccl} - &:& \text{The negative sign implies negative descent along the objective function.} \\ \nabla{f(x)} &:& \text{The objective function. Gradient.} \\ \nabla^2 f(x) &:& \text{The Hessian.} \end{array}$$
The Hessian is equivalent to a learning rate or step size. In order to not go uphill, the Hessian needs to be a positive learning rate. $$\begin{align*} \nabla^2 f > 0 &\quad \text{Leads to descent or minimisation} \\ \nabla^2 f < 0 &\quad \text{Leads to ascent or maximisation} \\ \end{align*}$$

Higher dimensional vector case

For minimisation, the Hessian needs to be strictly positive definite, $\nabla^2 f > 0$ (All eigenvalues are positive). Another way of writing it: $$ \nabla^2 f \in S^n_{++} $$
$$\begin{array}{ccl} S^n &:& \text{Symmetric matrix in n dimensions.} \\ ++ &:& \text{Strictly positive definite} \end{array}$$
If it were a single $+$, it would mean semi-positive definite meaning $\nabla^2 f \geq 0$.

In $\mathbb{R}^n$, by choosing the right coordinates (related to eigenvectors/values?), the Hessian matrix can be diagonalised, which decouples the objective function into $n$ scalar systems.

Convergence guarantees

If the Hessian of an objective function is globally positive definite, everywhere in $\mathbb{R}^n$, the function is said to be strongly convex. It's a big bowl. In which case, Newton's method is guaranteed to converge to the global optimum. $$ \nabla^2 f > 0 \, \rightarrow \, f(x)\text{ is strongly convex} \\ $$ In the strictly positive definite case, it's the global Optimum. In the semi-definite case, it's a global Optimum. In the latter, it is possible to have a flat bottom like a valley where all the points are equivalent. Weakly convex $\nabla^2 f \geq 0$ means that there are flat spots in the bowl.

When the objective function is strongly convex, guarantees can be provided of the runtime of Newton's method - predictions can be made of its runtime, how much sample rate is possible, all of which is important for real time control.
