Newton's method

$$ \nabla{f(x + \Delta{x})} \approx \nabla{f(x)} + \underbrace{\frac{\partial}{\partial{x}} \left(\nabla{f(x)}\right)}_{\nabla^2 f} \Delta{x} = 0 $$ Solving for $\Delta{x}$: $$ \Delta{x} = - \left(\nabla^2 f(x)\right)^{-1} \nabla{f(x)} $$ The new $x$ then becomes $x + \Delta{x}$. Repeat until convergence.

In this formulation, there is no notion of maximisation or minimisation. Newton's method is a local root-finding method that will converge to the closest fixed point (value of $x$ that causes the objective function to output a value as close to 0 as possible) to the initial guess; which could be a local minima, maxima or a saddle point.

Alternate interpretation

Newton's method on $\nabla{f}$ boils down to solving the first order Taylor expansion of $\nabla{f}$ by zeroing out the first term. Another interpretation: this is equivalent to fitting a local quadratic approximation to the function $f(x)$ and exactly minimising that local quadratic approximation.
