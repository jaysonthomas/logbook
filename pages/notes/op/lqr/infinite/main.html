Infinite Horizon LQR

Overview

For a time invariant problem where $A \; B \; Q \; R$ are not functions of $k$, i.e. remain constant, it turns out that $P \; K$ converge very quickly to steady state values as the algorithm goes backward in time. This is referred to as an infinite horizon LQR.

Example application: for stabilisation problems where the aim is to rest at some equilibrium point.

Time varying LQR is used for tracking problems where a trajectory is being tracked over time.

Aim

The aim is to solve for the steady state values of $P \; K$ and then use those as constants instead of dealing with time-varying $P \; K$. This is mainly used for stabilisation problems.

Math

Consider the backward recursion equations. In the infinite horizon limit, $P$ will not change. $$ P_{k+1} = P_k = P_\infty = P $$ The gain equation becomes: $$ K = (R + B^\top P B)^{-1} B^\top P A $$ Substituting this into the Riccati equation, it becomes: $$ P = Q + A^\top P A - A^\top P B (R + B^\top P B)^{-1} B^\top P A $$ This can be written in the form $f(P) = 0$ which becomes a root finding problem.

Practical solution

Because $P$ is a matrix, this is a system of nonlinear quadratic equations; it becomes a nonlinear root finding problem. In practice, generic root-finding methods are rarely used because the structure of the Riccati equation allows for more efficient, specialized algorithms.

The solution used is the Schur Method (Eigen-decomposition). In C++, Eigen combined with a DARE (Discrete-Time Algebraic Riccati Equation) solver library is used.
