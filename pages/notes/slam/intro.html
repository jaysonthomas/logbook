Intro

Overview

In SLAM, a robot must
  • Construct a map of the environment
  • While simultaneously localising itself (figuring out its pose) relative to the map
With noise in the robot's motions and measurements, the map and robot's pose will be uncertain, and the errors in the robot's pose estimates, and map will be correlated. The accuracy of the map depends on the accuracy of the localisation and vice versa. SLAM is often called the chicken or the egg problem because the map is needed for localisation, and the robot's pose is needed for mapping.

For robots to be useful to society, they must be able to move in environments that they've never seen before. For instance, it wouldn't be feasible to provide a robot vacuum cleaner with a map of all the furniture every time.

Categories of SLAM

SLAM algorithms generally fall into five categories.
  • EKF
  • Sparse extended information filter or SEIF
  • Extended information form or EIF

  • FastSLAM
    • It uses the particle filter approach along with the low dimensional extended kalman filter to solve the SLAM problem. This algorithm is then adapted to grid maps, which results in a grid based fastSLAM algorithm.

  • GraphSLAM
    • It uses constraints to represent relationships between robot poses and the environment, and then tries to resolve all of these constraints to create the most likely map given the data. An implementation of graphSLAM is called real time appearance based mapping, or RTABmap.
