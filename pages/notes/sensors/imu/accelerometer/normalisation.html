Normalisation

Overview

Normalisation strips away the magnitude (strength of the force) leaving only the direction of the acceleration.

To normalise an accelerometer reading, each axis component ($x, y, z$) is divided by the total magnitude (the Euclidean norm) $G$ of the vector.

$$\begin{align*} G = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2} \\ \hat{x} = \frac{x}{G}, \hat{y} = \frac{y}{G}, \hat{z} = \frac{z}{G} \end{align*}$$ The resulting vector $(\hat{x}, \hat{y}, \hat{z})$ will always have a length of exactly 1.

Use

  • Unit sphere perspective
    • Imagine an accelerometer is at the centre of a sphere with a radius of 1. The normalised vector is a vector pointing from the centre to a dot on the surface of that sphere. Tilt angles (pitch and roll) can be calculated using simple trigonometry from the dot's location.

  • Noise and Motion Filtering
    • By forcing a vector onto a unit sphere, it becomes much easier to compare accelerometer data with gyroscope data to filter out jitter or temporary movement spikes.
