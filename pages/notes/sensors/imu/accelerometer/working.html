Working principle

Overview

Accelerometers don't measure acceleration. According to Newton's 2nd law: $$ a = \frac{1}{m}F $$ The total acceleration on a vehicle is the sum of all forces $F$ acting on the vehicle. Accelerometers don't measure total acceleration, but specific acceleration which is the acceleration caused by all the forces acting on a vehicle except for gravity.

The output of an accelerometer is actually: $$ a_{accel} = \frac{1}{m}(F - F_g) $$

A full 3-axis accelerometer would need three of these in orthogonal directions.

Hardware setup

A simple accelerometer consists of a proof mass attached inside of some housing that is constrained to only move vertically. The proof mass is attached between the top and bottom of the housing with 2 springs. It is calibrated to be at the centre when the net external vertical force acting on the housing is $0$. The springs stretch/contract to keep the proof mass in the centre.
An accelerometer measures the difference between the upwards force provided by the upper spring and the downwards force provided by the lower spring. $$ F_{springs,net} = F_{s,up} - F_{s,down} $$

Example measurements

Resting on an object

When an accelerometer is sitting on a table, the housing and the proof mass have $0$ acceleration. To keep the proof mass in the centre, the net force from the springs on the accelerometer is upwards and equal to $mg$ - the accelerometer would measure a net acceleration upwards that's equal to $g$.

Free fall

If the sensor were in free fall, the housing and the proof mass would accelerate downwards at $9.8 m/s^2$. To keep the proof mass centered in the housing, the two springs would be pulling equally so the net force from the springs would be $0$ and the accelerometer would measure $0$ acceleration.
