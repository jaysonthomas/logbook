Common config parameters

Sensitivity

It configures the Measurement Range or Full Scale Range of the accelerometer, i.e. the range of forces the accelerometer can measure, say between -8g and +8g. If the device experiences a force greater than 8g (like a hard impact), the sensor will clip and not measure the excess.

In physics, sensitivity usually refers to the smallest change a sensor can detect which differs from this definition.

Output data rate

This is the speed at which the sensor samples acceleration and updates the data registers.

Resolution

This is the scale factor that defines how to convert the raw digital numbers (LSBs) coming out of the sensor into real-world physical units.

4.096 LSB/mg means that for every 1 milli-g of acceleration, the digital output increases by approximately 4 counts. Example: If a sensor reads a raw value of 4096, that equals 1000 mg or 1g.

For a gyroscope, the unit would be LSB/dps (degrees per second).

Bandwidth

Accelerometers are sensitive to high frequency vibrations. An internal low pass filter cuts off frequencies that are too fast for the ODR to capture accurately. ODR/4 means the bandwidth is set to 25% of the ODR.

Calculation: 50 Hz / 4 = 12.5 Hz. The sensor will filter out vibrations faster than 12.5 Hz.

Relationship between resolution and sensitivity

Because the sensor has a fixed number of digital "steps" (bits) available, changing the Range (Sensitivity) forces the Resolution (Scale Factor) to change. They are inversely proportional.

If you increase the range (e.g., to 16g) to measure bigger impacts, you stretch your available bits over a wider area. Your resolution gets worse (fewer LSBs per mg).If you decrease the range (e.g., to 2g) to measure tiny movements, your resolution gets better (more LSBs per mg).

Group delay

In some cases, an accelerometer doesn't just output raw electricity; it processes the data first. It runs the signal through a Digital Low-Pass Filter to smooth out vibrations and noise. The filter needs to collect a few samples of data to "average" them out or perform mathematical smoothing.

Gathering and processing these samples takes time. That waiting period is the Group Delay.

When the cut-off frequency of the sensor is lower (say ODR/4, narrow bandwidth), it takes more time to process the signal to make it smooth (23.6 ms vs 11.8ms for ODR/2).
