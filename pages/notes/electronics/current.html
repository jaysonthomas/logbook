Electronics: Current

Overview

Current is the translational motion of charged particles, driven by some electric field, through a conductive material.

It is measured in amperes - it is the count of the number of charges moving through a cross-section of a material per second.

Creation of a magnetic field

Based on Ampere's law, where there is a flow of current, there is a magnetic field around it whose strength is proportional to the current flow.

If a wire is coiled into a loop and a current is passed through it, the magnetic field will add constructively in the area enclosed by the loop. A current moving counterclockwise around a loop of wire will produce a magnetic field inside of it which points out of the screen.

When multiple loops are wound into a coil (a solenoid), the magnetic fields add constructively, creating a stronger field. A steady current (magnitude & direction) results in a steady magnetic field. This is just Ampere's law.

Changing magnetic fields

Changing current (magnitude or direction) results in a changing magnetic field (flux) which then induces a voltage (EMF), possibly generating a current in a nearby conductor. This phenomenon is called induction and is defined by Faraday's law.
