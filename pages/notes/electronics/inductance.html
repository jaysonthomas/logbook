Inductance

Basics

When an electric current flows through a wire or a coil (a wound wire), it produces a magnetic field around it. This magnetic field induces a voltage (or electromotive force) in the same coil or nearby conductors.

Inductance is like a resistance to changes in current. When the current in a circuit changes, the magnetic field around the conductor or coil changes as well. This change in magnetic field induces a voltage that opposes the change in current. Essentially, inductance resists sudden changes in the flow of electric current.

Inductance is a property of an electrical circuit that describes its ability to store and release energy in the form of a magnetic field. The unit of measurement is henry (H). Inductors are commonly used for filtering, energy storage, and signal processing.
