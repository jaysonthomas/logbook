Use

RK4 is useful to simulate a smooth set of ODEs.

Rather than fitting a line to a solution curve like the Euler methods, RK4 fits a cubic polinomial. It fits cubic splines over the time steps which is way more accurate than a line.

The accuracy achieved is of higher value than the computational cost.

Overview of the method

$$\begin{align*} x_{k+1} &= f_d(x_k) \\\\ K_1 &= f(x_k) \\ K_2 &= f(x_k + \frac{h}{2} K_1) \\ K_3 &= f(x_k + \frac{h}{2} K_2) \\ K_4 &= f(x_k + \frac{h}{2} K_3) \\ \\ x_{k+1} &= x_k + \frac{h}{6} \left(K_1 + 2K_2 + 2K_3 + K_4\right) \end{align*}$$
$$\begin{array}{ccl} f &:& \text{The continuous function} \\ \end{array}$$
Evaluate $f$ twice at half-way points and then finally take a full step. This set of equations is the result of doing quadrature with a cubic spline. This is approximating an interval with a cubic polynomial.

The final result is a weighted combination of $K$s with the middle terms weighted more heavily.

Problems

As long as the time steps are small 0.2ms (5 Hz), the integrator behaves well. As the time step gets bigger, it adds artificial damping. Beyond a certain point, it does weird things and blows up.

The method

$$\begin{align*} \newcommand{\oState}[1]{\begin{bmatrix}\dot\theta_{#1} \\ \ddot\theta_{#1} \end{bmatrix}} \newcommand{\iState}{\begin{bmatrix}\theta \\ \dot\theta \end{bmatrix}} \newcommand{\iPrevOutput}[1]{ \begin{bmatrix} \theta + \dot\theta_{#1} \cdot \frac{h}{2} \\ \dot\theta + \ddot\theta_{#1} \cdot \frac{h}{2} \\ \end{bmatrix} } \newcommand{\iPrevOutputTwo}[1]{ \begin{bmatrix} \theta + \dot\theta_{#1} \cdot h \\ \dot\theta + \ddot\theta_{#1} \cdot h \\ \end{bmatrix} } k_1 = \oState{_1} &= f(t, \iState) \\ k_2 = \oState{_2} &= f(t + \frac{h}{2}, \iPrevOutput{1}) \\ k_3 = \oState{_3} &= f(t + \frac{h}{2}, \iPrevOutput{2}) \\ k_4 = \oState{_4} &= f(t + h, \iPrevOutputTwo{3}) \\\\ \theta &= \theta + (\dot\theta_1 + 2\dot\theta_2 + 2\dot\theta_3 + \dot\theta_4) \cdot \frac{h}{6} \\ \dot\theta &= \dot\theta + (\ddot\theta_1 + 2\ddot\theta_2 + 2\ddot\theta_3 + \ddot\theta_4) \cdot \frac{h}{6} \\ t &= t + h \end{align*}$$
