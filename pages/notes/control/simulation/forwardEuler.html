Forward Euler integration

Math

$$ x_{k+1} = \underbrace{x_k + h \, f(x_k, u_k)}_{f_d(x_k, u_k)} $$
$$\begin{array}{cclc} h &:& \text{Time step} \\ f_d &:& \text{Discrete function} \\ f &:& \text{Continuous function} \end{array}$$
A solution curve is approximated by taking a small step along the tangent lines on the curve. The linear approximation always overshoots which leads to an output that is physically nonsensical.

Error

The error in Forward Euler comes from the fact that a straight line cannot follow a curve. Consider what happens locally when the curvature of a function (second derivative and hence the first) changes.

Concave up

A bowl. Consider the curve from the equilibrium point (base of the bowl or minimum point) onwards.

$\ddot{y} > 0$. From a velocity perspective, the curve bends upwards. The tangent line (the "old" slope) keeps going straight (undershoots) while the real solution goes up.

Concave down

Upside-down bowl. Consider the curve from the equilibrium point (upside-down base of the bowl or maximum point) onwards.

$\ddot{y} < 0$. From a velocity perspective, the curve bends downwards. The tangent line (the "old" slope) keeps going straight (overshoots) while the real solution drops.

Overshoot

Forward euler can both overshoot and undershoot. Overshoot gets more attention because of oscillatory systems like a spring or pendulum. In these systems, the velocity solution is a sine or cosine wave.
  • As a pendulum swings toward the center, it is concave down relative to its velocity.
  • Forward Euler uses the high velocity from the start of the step and applies it for the whole duration.
  • By the time the step ends, the "real" pendulum should have slowed down because of gravity, but the Euler pendulum kept going at the higher speed.
  • This results in an overshoot of the displacement.
Forward Euler is blind to the change in slope during the interval $\Delta t$. It assumes the world is a series of perfectly straight lines, but physics is a world of curves.

Sort

Consider a linear system with its system output $y$ as below: $$\begin{align*} \dot{x} &= Ax + Bu \\ y &= Cx \end{align*}$$ A state sequence is discretised using the Forward Euler method as follows: $$ \dot{x} \approx \frac{x_{k+1} - x_k}{h} $$ $h$ is a discretisation time constant (a small real number)
$k$ denotes a discrete time instant after $kh$ time steps. $k = 0,1,2,..$
$x_k$ is an approximation of the state vector at the time instant $kh$, that is an approximation of $x(kh)$
$x(0)$ is assumed to be known.

The state-space model becomes: $$\begin{align} \label{eq:fEuler} x_{k+1} &= \underbrace{(I + hA)}_{A_f}x_k + \underbrace{hB}_{B_f}u_k \\ y_k &= Cx_k \nonumber \end{align}$$ $y_k, u_k$ are defined in the same manner as the discretised state variable $x_k$.

The discretised state sequence $x_1, x_2...$ is obtained by propagating \ref{eq:fEuler} from the initial condition $x_0$ using the control input sequence $u_0, u_1, u_2,\ldots$.

This approach might produce numerical instabilities within the discretised state vector $x_k$ where the state vector might diverge to infinity.
