Description

Intuition

A Forward Euler step looks at the current position and velocity to decide where to be in the next second.

Backward Euler looks for a point $x_{k+1}$ such that, if it were already at that point and looked backward, the slope would point exactly back to where it started ($x_k$). The future state is consistent with the velocity at that future state.

Imagine driving a car on a curvy road:
  • Forward Euler looks at the steering wheel's current position and floors it for 5 seconds. If the road curves sharply, it will drive off the cliff before it can adjust.

  • Backward Euler finds a spot on the road 5 seconds ahead and calculates exactly how the steering wheel should be turned at that spot so that the path to get there is perfectly smooth. It is harder to calculate, but much less likely to crash.

Math

$$\begin{equation} x_{k+1} = x_k + hf(x_{k+1}) \label{eq:1} \end{equation}$$ Rearranging into the general backward euler form: $$\begin{align*} x_{k+1} - x_k - hf(x_{k+1}) &= 0 \\ f(x_{k+1}, x_k, u_k) &= 0 \end{align*}$$ The difference between RHS and LHS in $\ref{eq:1}$ is the residual.
$$\begin{array}{ccl} x_{k+1} &:& \text{Unknown future state} \\ x_k &:& \text{Known current state} \\ h &:& \text{Time step} \\ f(x_{k+1}) &:& \text{The derivative (velocity) at the future position.} \\ \end{array}$$
Since $x_{k+1}$ appears on both sides of the equation, it cannot be calculated directly. An iterative solver will be required to hunt for the value of $x_{k+1}$ such that it makes the equation true. This is why it is called an implicit method.
