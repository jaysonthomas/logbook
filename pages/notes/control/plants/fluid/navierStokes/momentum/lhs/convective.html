Convective Acceleration

Overview

This captures the change in velocity as the fluid moves to a new location. For example, if a slow-moving river enters a narrow gorge, the water must speed up simply because it is moving into a tighter space, even if the flow rate is steady. $$ (u \cdot \nabla)u $$ $u \cdot \nabla$ is not being multiplied by $u$. Just as $\frac{d}{dx}$ is an operator applied to/acting on $f(x)$ in $\frac{d}{dx} f(x)$, $u \cdot \nabla$ is an operator applied to $u$.
  • $u \cdot \nabla$: Differentiate along the flow direction.
  • $u$: The quantity being differentiated.
$$ \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta \text{position}} \times \frac{\Delta \text{position}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta t} $$ The spatial gradient of $u$ weighted by how fast the fluid is traversing that space results in an acceleration in the direction of motion.

It's a chain rule in disguise.

$$ \frac{du}{dt} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial z}\frac{dz}{dt} = (u \cdot \nabla)u $$
