Balance of moments about the centre of mass

The advantage of computing the balance of moments about A is that $F_1$ and $F_2$ don't create any moments about the point. Hoping to never have to evaluate them is futile, since they pop up in the sum of forces acting on the pole anyway. The number of terms in the sum of torques equation does reduce though. $$\begin{align*} \newcommand{eqRef}[1]{\tag{#1} \label{eq:#1}} \sum \tau^G_{\hat{k}} &= \frac{d}{dt} H^G \\ &= F_2\,l \,\hat{k} \\ &= \frac{d}{dt} \biggl(I^G \begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 0 \\ \omega_z\end{bmatrix}\biggr) \\ &= I^G_{zz} \, \ddot\theta \, \hat{k} \\ F_2 &= \frac{J \, \ddot\theta}{l} \\ \ddot\theta &= \frac{lF_2}{J} \eqRef{3} \end{align*}$$ $\frac{d}{dt} H^G$ is the time rate of change of angular momentum about G.

$\sum \tau^G_{\hat{k}}$ is the sum of external torques about G. The only external torque is caused by $F_2$ about a moment arm of length $l$. Gravity causes no torque since it is acting downwards at G. The torque due to $F_2$ is positive when $F_2$ causes a rotation in the counter-clockwise $\hat{k}$ direction.

$I^G$ is the mass moment of inertia of the pole about G. Since the only term that matters is that in the direction of $\hat{k}$, only $I^G_{zz}$ is considered. It is similar to that of a uniform rod.
