Cartpole: Derivation of equations of motion

Balance of forces about the centre of mass

Cart

A is the point at which the mass of the cart is concentrated. $$\begin{align} \newcommand{\WaG}{^{\scriptsize{W}\hspace{-0.8em}}a^{\scriptsize{G}}} \sum F_\hat{i}^{\scriptsize{A}} &= m^{\scriptsize{A}} \ddot{x} \,\hat{i} \nonumber \\ &= (-kx -b\dot{x} + F_2\, cos\theta + F_1\, sin\theta) \,\hat{i} \label{eq:iHatCartForces} \end{align}$$

Pole

$$\begin{align} \sum F_\hat{d}^{\scriptsize{G}} &= m^{\scriptsize{G}} \,\WaG \,\hat{d} \nonumber \\ &= -F_1 \,\hat{d} + m^{\scriptsize{G}}g\,cos\theta \,\hat{d} \label{eq:dHatPoleForces} \\ \sum F_\hat{q}^{\scriptsize{G}} &= m^{\scriptsize{G}} \,\WaG \,\hat{q} \nonumber \\ &= -F_2 \,\hat{q} - m^{\scriptsize{G}}g\,sin\theta \,\hat{q} \label{eq:qHatPoleForces} \end{align}$$ $^{\scriptsize{W}\hspace{-0.4em}}a^{\scriptsize{G}}\,\hat{d}$ is the acceleration of the centre of gravity/mass of the pole w.r.t. the world frame in the $\hat{d}$ direction.

Balance of moments about the centre of mass

The advantage of computing the balance of moments about A is that $F_1$ and $F_2$ don't create any moments about the point. Hoping to never have to evaluate them is futile, since they pop up in the sum of forces about $\hat{d}$ and $\hat{q}$ anyway. The number of terms in the sum of torques equation does reduce though in the former case. $$\begin{align} \sum \tau^G_{\hat{k}} &= \frac{d}{dt} H^G \nonumber \nonumber \\ &= F_2\,l \,\hat{k} \nonumber \\ &= \frac{d}{dt} \biggl(I^G \begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 0 \\ \omega_z\end{bmatrix}\biggr) \nonumber \\ &= I^G_{zz} \, \ddot\theta \, \hat{k} \nonumber \\ &= \frac{m_G\, l^2}{12}\,\ddot\theta \, \hat{k} \label{eq:kHatPoleMoments} \end{align}$$ $\frac{d}{dt} H^G$ is the time rate of change of angular momentum about G.

$\sum \tau^G_{\hat{k}}$ is the sum of external torques about G. The only external torque is caused by $F_2$ about a moment arm of length $l$. Gravity causes no torque since it is acting downwards at G. The torque due to $F_2$ is positive when $F_2$ causes a rotation in the counter-clockwise $\hat{k}$ direction.

$I^G$ is the mass moment of inertia of the pole about G. Since the only term that matters is that in the direction of $\hat{k}$, only $I^G_{zz}$ is considered. It is similar to that of a uniform rod.

Equations of motion

Isolating $F_1, F_2$ in equations \ref{eq:dHatPoleForces} and \ref{eq:qHatPoleForces}, and substituting them in \ref{eq:kHatPoleMoments} and \ref{eq:iHatCartForces}.
