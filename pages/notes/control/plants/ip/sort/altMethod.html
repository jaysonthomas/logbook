Alternate method for computing pole-kinematics

Linear velocity

Translational motion

The centre of mass of the pole moves at the same velocity as that of the cart, $\dot{x}$, in the $\hat{i}$ direction. $$ \newcommand{\wrt}[4]{^{\scriptsize{#1}\hspace{-#2em}}{#3}^{\scriptsize{#4}}} \wrt{W}{0.3}{v}{A} = \dot{x}\, \hat{i} $$

Rotational motion

A linear velocity due to rotation is given by $v = r\omega$ and is directed perpendicular to the radius, formed by the length of the pole. $$\begin{align*} \newcommand{\avg}{\wrt{A}{0.1}{v}{G}} \newcommand{\arg}{\wrt{A}{0.5}{r}{G}} \newcommand{\wwg}{\wrt{A}{0.3}{\omega}{G}} \newcommand{\wwgTwo}{\wrt{W}{0.5}{\omega}{G}} \avg &= \,\avg\Bigr|_{\wwg=0} + \bigl(\,\wwgTwo \times \arg\,\bigr) \\ &= 0 + \bigl(\omega_z\,\hat{k} \times l\,\hat{d}\,\bigr) \\ &= l\dot\theta\,\hat{q} \end{align*}$$ $\avg\Bigr|_{\wwg=0}$ is the velocity of G as seen from A when G is not rotating. Since G does not have any linear motions, the term is $0$.

$^{\scriptsize{A}}r^{\scriptsize{G}}$ is the position vector between the 2 points.

Combining the 2 components

$$ \newcommand{\wvg}{\wrt{W}{0.3}{v}{G}} \wvg = \dot{x}\,\hat{i} + l\dot\theta\,\hat{q} $$

Linear acceleration

Refer here for derivatives of unit vectors that rotate [example: $\hat{q}$]. $$\begin{align*} \newcommand{\wdt}{ ^{\raise{2.5ex} \scriptsize{W} \hspace{-1.5em}} {\frac{d}{dt}} } \wrt{W}{0.5}{a}{G} &= \wdt\,\wvg \\ &= \ddot{x}\,\hat{i} + l\ddot\theta\,\hat{q} + l\dot\theta \frac{d}{dt} \hat{q} \\ &= \ddot{x}\,\hat{i} + l\ddot\theta\,\hat{q} - l\dot\theta^2\,\hat{d} \\ \end{align*}$$
In terms of $\hat{d}$ and $\hat{q}$ unit vectors: $$ a_G = (\ddot{x}\,sin\theta - l\dot\theta^2)\,\hat{d} + (\ddot{x}\,cos\theta + l\ddot\theta)\,\hat{q} $$

Shortcut for computing linear acceleration

There are always 5 potential terms when evaluating the acceleration of a point on a rigid body that is both translating and rotating. The reference frame attached to the rigid body is both translating and rotating. $$\begin{align*} \newcommand{\wrt}[4]{^{\scriptsize{#1}\hspace{-#2em}}{#3}^{\scriptsize{#4}}} \wrt{W}{0.5}{a}{G} &= \wrt{W}{1.0}{a}{A} + \wrt{A}{0.5}{a}{G}\Bigr|_{\wrt{W}{0.1}{\omega}{}=0} + \wrt{W}{0.7}{\dot\omega}{} \times \wrt{A}{0.3}{r}{G} + \wrt{W}{0.7}{\omega}{} \times (\wrt{W}{0.4}{\omega}{} \times \wrt{A}{0.5}{r}{G}) + 2\,\wrt{W}{0.3}{\dot\omega}{} \times (\wrt{A}{0.1}{v}{G}\Bigr|_{\wrt{W}{0.1}{\omega}{}=0}) \end{align*}$$ $\wrt{W}{1.0}{a}{A}$ is the translational acceleration.

$\wrt{A}{0.5}{a}{G}\Bigr|_{\wrt{W}{0.1}{\omega}{}=0}$ is the acceleration of point G relative to A, if one was evaluating it when standing on the object at G.

$\wrt{W}{0.7}{\dot\omega}{} \times \wrt{A}{0.3}{r}{G}$ is the eulerian term which is $\ddot\theta$ multiplied by the distance between G and A.

$\wrt{W}{0.7}{\omega}{} \times (\wrt{W}{0.4}{\omega}{} \times \wrt{A}{0.5}{r}{G})$ is the centripetal term.

$2\,\wrt{W}{0.3}{\dot\omega}{} \times (\wrt{A}{0.1}{v}{G}\Bigr|_{\wrt{W}{0.1}{\omega}{}=0})$ is the coriolis term which requires motion of the point G relative to A. $$ \wrt{W}{0.5}{a}{G} = \ddot{x}\,\hat{i} + 0 + l\ddot\theta\,\hat{q} - l\dot\theta^2\,\hat{d} + 0 $$
