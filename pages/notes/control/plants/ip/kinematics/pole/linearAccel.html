Linear acceleration of a pole attached to a cart

In the world reference frame

The derivative of the pole's linear velocity yields: $$\begin{align*} \newcommand{\wrt}[4]{^{\scriptsize{#1}\hspace{#2em}}{#3}^{\scriptsize{#4}}} \wrt{A}{0}{\ddot{x}}{G} &= l(\ddot\theta \, cos\theta - \dot\theta^2 \, sin\theta) \, \hat{i} \\ \wrt{A}{0}{\ddot{y}}{G} &= l(\ddot\theta \, sin\theta + \dot\theta^2 \, cos\theta) \, \hat{j} \\ \wrt{W}{-0.5}{a}{G} &= \dot{x}\, \hat{i} + l(\ddot\theta \, cos\theta - \dot\theta^2 \, sin\theta) \, \hat{i} + l(\ddot\theta \, sin\theta + \dot\theta^2 \, cos\theta) \, \hat{j} \\ \end{align*}$$

In the body reference frame

Considering:
  • Derivatives of unit vectors that rotate
  • Representation of a vector in a rotated coordinate system
$$\begin{align*} \newcommand{\wrt}[4]{^{\scriptsize{#1}\hspace{#2em}}{#3}^{\scriptsize{#4}}} \wrt{W}{-0.5}{a}{G} &= \ddot{x}\,\hat{i} + l\ddot\theta\,\hat{q} + l\dot\theta \frac{d}{dt} \hat{q} \\ &= \ddot{x}\,\hat{i} + l\ddot\theta\,\hat{q} - l\dot\theta^2\,\hat{d} \\ &= (\ddot{x}\,sin\theta - l\dot\theta^2)\,\hat{d} + (\ddot{x}\,cos\theta + l\ddot\theta)\,\hat{q} \end{align*}$$

Shortcut for computing linear acceleration

There are always 5 potential terms when evaluating the acceleration of a point on a rigid body that is both translating and rotating. The reference frame attached to the rigid body is both translating and rotating. $$\begin{align*} \newcommand{\wrt}[4]{^{\scriptsize{#1}\hspace{-#2em}}{#3}^{\scriptsize{#4}}} \wrt{W}{0.5}{a}{G} &= \wrt{W}{1.0}{a}{A} + \wrt{A}{0.5}{a}{G}\Bigr|_{\wrt{W}{0.1}{\omega}{}=0} + \wrt{W}{0.7}{\dot\omega}{} \times \wrt{A}{0.3}{r}{G} + \wrt{W}{0.7}{\omega}{} \times (\wrt{W}{0.4}{\omega}{} \times \wrt{A}{0.5}{r}{G}) + 2\,\wrt{W}{0.3}{\dot\omega}{} \times (\wrt{A}{0.1}{v}{G}\Bigr|_{\wrt{W}{0.1}{\omega}{}=0}) \end{align*}$$ $\wrt{W}{1.0}{a}{A}$ is the translational acceleration.

$\wrt{A}{0.5}{a}{G}\Bigr|_{\wrt{W}{0.1}{\omega}{}=0}$ is the acceleration of point G relative to A, if one was evaluating it when standing on the object at G.

$\wrt{W}{0.7}{\dot\omega}{} \times \wrt{A}{0.3}{r}{G}$ is the eulerian term which is $\ddot\theta$ multiplied by the distance between G and A.

$\wrt{W}{0.7}{\omega}{} \times (\wrt{W}{0.4}{\omega}{} \times \wrt{A}{0.5}{r}{G})$ is the centripetal term.

$2\,\wrt{W}{0.3}{\dot\omega}{} \times (\wrt{A}{0.1}{v}{G}\Bigr|_{\wrt{W}{0.1}{\omega}{}=0})$ is the coriolis term which requires motion of the point G relative to A. $$ \wrt{W}{0.5}{a}{G} = \ddot{x}\,\hat{i} + 0 + l\ddot\theta\,\hat{q} - l\dot\theta^2\,\hat{d} + 0 $$
