Monorotor dynamics

Forces

Decomposition

Thrust and gravity

A monorotor has only one rotor and therefore only one thrust vector pointing upwards in the positive $z$ direction. This will cause an acceleration in the z direction. $$ F_z = mg - F_{thrust} $$ $F_z$ is the net force on the vehicle. To hover ($F_z=0$), the thrust force upwards would need to be equal to the force that gravity exerts downwards.

Drag

A quadrotor as a whole and the individual propellers experience drag.

In practice, drag is treated as a disturbance when designing quadrotor controllers. It isn't modeled explicitly since:

  • Its effects are relatively small at lower vehicle and wind speeds
  • It is difficult to identify the correct drag model

Linear translational acceleration

$$\begin{align*} \ddot{z} &= g - \frac{F_{thrust}}{m} \\ &= g - \frac{u}{m} \end{align*}$$

Translational equilibrium

When a monorotor is hovering, it is in translational equilibrium.

Moments

Decomposition

[Monorotor spinning sim video]

A Reaction moment is a rotational moment that is an opposite reaction to some other rotational motion.

In addition to causing a thrust force, a rotating propeller also introduces a reaction moment. A rotor that's spinning clockwise will produce a moment that causes the body to spin counter-clockwise.

Rotational acceleration

For a monorotor where only the z-axis is considered, the torque equation would be: $$ M_z = I_z \ddot\psi $$ $\psi$ is the yaw angle.

Rotational equilibrium

It's impossible for a monorotor to sit truly motionless in the sky because it can never be in rotational equilibrium.
