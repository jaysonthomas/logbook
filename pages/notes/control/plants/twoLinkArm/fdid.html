Forward/Inverse Dynamics

Inverse Dynamics

The equation of motion of a robot also called the Inverse dynamics equation is given as: $$\begin{align*} \tau &= M(q)\ddot{q} + C(q, \dot{q}) + G(q) \\ &= ID(q, \dot{q}, \ddot{q}) \end{align*}$$ where $M$ is the joint-space inertia matrix, $C$ is the vector of Coriolis and centrifugal forces and $G$ is the gravity vector in the joint space. $M$ is a positive definite symmetric matrix. $C$ has $\dot{q}$ in every term so $C = 0$ if $\dot{q}=0$.

An inverse dynamics function gets $q, \dot{q}, \ddot{q}$ as inputs and computes the torques required to achieve a desired trajectory. It is used for control.

Forward Dynamics

A forward dynamics function gets $q, \dot{q}, \tau$ as inputs and computes $\ddot{q}$ as an output by first computing $M, C, G$: $$\begin{align*} \ddot{q} &= M^{-1} (\tau - C(q, \dot{q}) - G(q)) \\ &= FD(q, \dot{q}, \tau) \end{align*}$$ FD is used for simulation purposes. FD outputs joint accelerations due to some applied torques which can then be integrated over time to compute the evolution of $q$.
