Linearisation Process

Taylor expansion at an equilibrium point

Consider the system has an equilibrium point at $x^*$. If the system starts there, it stays there, i.e. the next state around an equilibrium point is the same as the current state. $$ x^* = f_d(x^*) $$ Consider a very small perturbation $\Delta x_k$ is applied to the system near the equilibrium point. Any smooth function $f_d(x_k)$ can then be expanded around a fixed point $x^*$ using the Taylor Series: $$ f_d(x_{k+1}) = f_d(x^*) + \frac{\partial f_d}{\partial x} \bigg|_{x^*} (x_k - x^*) + \text{Higher Order Terms (H.O.T.)} $$

Linearisation

As part of linearisation, ignoring the H.O.T., the equation simplifies to: $$ x_{k+1} \approx x^* + A_d \, (x_k - x^*) $$ $A_d = \frac{\partial f_d}{\partial x} \bigg|_{x=x^*}$ is the Jacobian matrix.

Redefinition of the perturbation

Redefining the small shift/deviation/error from the fixed point:
$$\begin{array}{cclc} \Delta x_k = (x_k - x^*) &:& \text{How far the system is from the target now.} \\ \Delta x_{k+1} = (x_{k+1} - x^*) &:& \text{How far the system will be in the next step} \end{array}$$
The above equation simplifies to the following linear mapping: $$ \Delta x_{k+1} = A_d \Delta x_k $$

Origin shift

Redefining the coordinate system so that the fixed point $x^*$ is at $0$. If $x^* = 0$, then $\Delta x_k$ is just $x_k$, and the equation simplifies to: $$ x_{k+1} = A_d x_k $$
