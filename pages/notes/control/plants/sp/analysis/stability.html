System Jacobian Eigen values

Asymptotic stability analysis

System

Considering the dynamics of an uncontrolled simple pendulum, i.e. no applied torque: $$ f(x) = \dot{x} = \begin{bmatrix} \dot{\theta} \\ \frac{-g \,sin\theta}{l} \end{bmatrix} $$

Jacobian

The Jacobian (Taking the 1st column: derivative w.r.t. $\theta$, 2nd column: derivative w.r.t. $\dot\theta$): $$ \frac{\partial{f}}{\partial{x}} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ \frac{-g \,sin\theta}{l} & 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

Eigen values

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix, it is easy to compute the eigen values (taking the determinant etc.). Computing the eigen values for the pendulum at the 2 equilibrium points:

At $\theta = \pi$

$$\begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\Bigr|_{\theta=\pi} &= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ \frac{g}{l} & 0 \end{bmatrix} \\ \text{eigvals}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right) &= \pm \sqrt{\frac{g}{l}} \\ & \implies \text{unstable} \end{align*}$$ Since one of the eigenvalues is positive, the system is considered to be unstable. Intuitively, the pendulum is not stable when pointed up.

At $\theta = 0$

$$\begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\Bigr|_{\theta=0} &= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ \frac{-g}{l} & 0 \end{bmatrix} \\ \text{eigvals}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right) &= \pm i \sqrt{\frac{g}{l}} \\ & \implies \text{undamped oscillation} \end{align*}$$ The real part is 0, i.e. there is no damping. From a dynamics perspective, the pair of pure imaginary values implies a purely undamped system that will oscillate forever.

A complex value, from euler's formula, means a complex exponential; The imaginary part contains sines and cosines. So, the oscillations makes sense. The frequency of the oscillation is $\sqrt{\frac{g}{l}}$ which is the oscillatory period of a pendulum.

From a stability-analysis perspective, nothing conclusive can be said about the stability of the system. Locally, it has undamped oscillations in a tiny neighborhood of the origin. If the system is then excited, it will excite high order terms in the tailor expansion. Since, there's no damping, it could be worrying. Some other kind of analysis would be required.

The pendulum is not asymptotically stable in the downward hanging position - it doesn't asymptotically go back to the equilibrium point. It hangs around and does an oscillatory behaviour. So, it is stable in a general, lyapunov sense since it stays in some bounded neighbourhood.

To make this stable, damping would need to be added with the controller. The torque input can be used to shape the dynamics to make it act like there's a damper. E.g. adding $u = -k_d \, \dot\theta$ results in strictly negative real part.
