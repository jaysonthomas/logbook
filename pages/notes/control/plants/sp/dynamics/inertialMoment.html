Inertial Moment

Overview

Inertial moment or the torque due to inertia of a rotating pendulum is a measure of its resistance to changes in its angular motion. $$ \newcommand{\wrt}[3]{#1^{\hspace{#2em}\scriptsize{#3}}} (M^{\hspace{-0.2em}\scriptsize{A}}_i) \,\hat{k} = -\wrt{J}{-0.2}{A} \ddot\theta $$

Moment of inertia

Based on parallel axis theorem: $$\begin{align*} \wrt{J}{-0.1}{G} &= m_p \cdot r_p^2 \\ &= 0 \\ \wrt{J}{-0.2}{A} &= \cancel{\wrt{J}{-0.1}{G}} + m_pl^2 \end{align*}$$
$$\begin{array}{ccl} G &:& \text{Centre of mass of a simple pendulum is at the point mass} \\ A &:& \text{Pivot point - the axis of rotation} \\ \wrt{J}{-0.1}{G} &:& \text{Moment of inertia about } G \\ \wrt{J}{-0.2}{A} &:& \text{Moment of inertia about } A \\ l &:& \text{Distance between } A \text{ and } G \end{array}$$
$r_p$ is the distance of the point mass $m_p$ from the centre of mass, which is 0 in this case since the pendulum's mass is treated as concentrated at a single position [Ref].
