Laplace Transform: Example

Setup

Consider the function $u(t)e^{-t}$ and working through the different real exponents $\sigma$, i.e. the Laplace transform.
Laplace transform: $$ F(s) = \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} \underbrace{u(t)e^{-t}}_{\scriptsize\text{Impulse response function}} \overbrace{e^{-st}}^{\scriptsize\text{Points in s-plane}}dt $$ The time domain function is probed with $e^{-st}$ across the entire s-plane to see what it's made of.

Different real exponent values

$\sigma = -1$

To find the Laplace transform:
  • Multiply the time domain signal $u(t) \cdot e^{-t}$ by the exponential $e^{-\sigma t}$ when $\sigma=-1$
  • Take the fourier transform of the result
$$\begin{align*} F(s) &= \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} \left[ u(t) \cdot e^{-t} \cdot e^{--1t} \right] e^{-j\omega t} dt \\ &= \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} u(t) e^{-j\omega t} dt \end{align*}$$ The fourier transform of a step function $u(t)$ does not converge, i.e. it is outside of the region of convergence, however, just barely.

Other $\sigma$ values

It is possible to choose $\sigma$ values that:
  • Produce a near step function that is slowly decaying
    • In this case, the fourier transform will converge.

  • Produce a near step function that is slowly growing
    • In this case, the Fourier transform is even further from converging than just the simple step function.

Special $\sigma$ value

At the location $\sigma=-1$ in the s-plane, the integral exists right on the cusp of converging and diverging.
At $s=-1$, for $\omega=0$ and $\sigma=-1$, the result of the fourier transform goes to infinity.
Interestingly, both the impulse response function in the time domain and the interesting point in the s-plane contain $e^{-t}$.
