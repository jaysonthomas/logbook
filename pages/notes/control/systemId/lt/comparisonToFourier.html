Laplace Transform: Comparison to Fourier

Definition

Fourier transform maps time to frequency. It decomposes a signal or function into sinusoids. $$ F(\omega) = \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} f(t) e^{-j\omega t} dt $$ Laplace transform maps time to $s$. It decomposes a signal or function into both sinusoids and exponentials. $$ F(s) = \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} f(t) e^{-st} dt $$ The difference between the two is that, the real component $\sigma$ is added to the equation: $$\begin{align*} F(s) &= \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} f(t) e^{-st} dt \\ &= \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} f(t) e^{-(\sigma + j\omega)t} dt \\ &= \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} \left[ f(t) e^{-\sigma t} \right] e^{-j\omega t} dt \\ \end{align*}$$ The laplace transform:
  • Multiplies the original time domain function $f(t)$ by an exponential term $e^{-\sigma t}$
  • Takes the fourier transform of the product $f(t) \cdot e^{-\sigma t}$

Equality

On the imaginary axis, where $\sigma = 0$, the laplace transform for values of $s$ along this line is exactly equal to the fourier transform: $$\begin{align*} F(s) &= \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} \left[ f(t) e^{-\sigma t} \right] e^{-j\omega t} dt \\ &= \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} \left[ f(t) e^{-0 t} \right] e^{-j\omega t} dt \\ &= \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} f(t) e^{-j\omega t} dt \end{align*}$$

Graphically

The results of the Fourier transform are a set of 2D numbers that represent magnitude and phase for a given frequency. The results of the Laplace transform are still the same 2D numbers, with an additional real component, plotted on a 3D s-plane.
