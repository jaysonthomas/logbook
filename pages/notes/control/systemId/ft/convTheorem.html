Convolution Theorem

Statement

Multiplying 2 functions in the frequency domain accomplishes the same result as convolution (or the convolution integral) in the time domain. For example, given the frequency domain representation of a system's impulse response and an arbitrary input signal, the system's response to the input can be calculated by just multiplying the two.

Multiplication of 2 transfer functions takes advantage of the convolution theorem.

Background

Fourier transform: $$ F(\omega) = \int^\infty_{-\infty}f(t)e^{-j\omega t}dt $$ Convolution integral: $$ (f*g)(t) = \int^\infty_{-\infty}f(\tau)g(t-\tau)d\tau $$

Derivation

Taking the fourier transform of the convolution integral: $$ \mathcal{F}((f*g)(t)) = \int^\infty_{-\infty} \left[ \int^\infty_{-\infty}f(\tau)g(t-\tau)d\tau \right] e^{-j\omega t} dt $$ From double integral basics, the integral can be rearranged as: $$ \mathcal{F}((f*g)(t)) = \int^\infty_{-\infty} \left[ f(\tau) \int^\infty_{-\infty} g(t-\tau) e^{-j\omega t} dt \right] d\tau $$ From Fourier shift theorem, the equation can be rewritten as: $$ \mathcal{F}((f*g)(t)) = \int^\infty_{-\infty} f(\tau) G(\omega) e^{-j\omega \tau} d\tau $$ $G(\omega)$ is constant w.r.t. $\tau$, hence can be pulled out: $$ \mathcal{F}((f*g)(t)) = F(\omega) \cdot G(\omega) $$
