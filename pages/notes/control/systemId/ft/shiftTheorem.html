Fourier Transform: Shift Theorem

Setup

Consider an arbitrary function $f(t)$ shifted or delayed by time $\tau$.
The aim is to find the fourier transform of the shifted function.

Derivation

The original function

Fourier transform of $f(t)$: $$ \mathcal{F}(f(t)) = \int^\infty_{-\infty} f(t) e^{-j\omega t}dt $$

$\mathcal{F}$ denotes taking the fourier transform of what's inside the parentheses.

The shifted function

The height and width ($dt == d(t-\tau)$) of the approximate rectangular areas of the 2 functions (shifted & non-shifted) are the same. Hence, replacing $t$ with $t-\tau$: $$\begin{equation} \mathcal{F}(f(t-\tau)) = \int^\infty_{-\infty} f(t-\tau) e^{-j\omega t}dt \label{eq:shiftedFunc} \end{equation}$$

Shifted function in terms of the original

The timing needs to be fixed by adjusting the $e^{-j\omega t}$ term, which is of a different time frame compared to $f(t-\tau)$. To get the time aligned, the above expression is multiplied by $1$ (which doesn't change anything), represented as $e^{-j\omega \tau}e^{j\omega \tau}$. These exponentials are a function of $\tau$ and therefore constant with respect to $t$. $$\begin{align*} \mathcal{F}(f(t-\tau)) &= \int^\infty_{-\infty} f(t-\tau) e^{-j\omega t}e^{-j\omega \tau} e^{j\omega \tau} dt \\ &= e^{-j\omega \tau}\int^\infty_{-\infty} f(t-\tau) e^{-j\omega (t-\tau)} dt \\ \end{align*}$$ Replacing time frame $t-\tau$ with $T$ and adjusting the integral limits accordingly (since the integration is over all time, a function that is shifted by a finite value has no impact on the limits). $$\begin{align*} \mathcal{F}(f(t-\tau)) &= e^{-j\omega \tau} \underbrace{\int^\infty_{-\infty} f(T) e^{-j\omega T}dT}_ {\scriptsize\text{Fourier transform}} \\ &= e^{-j\omega \tau} \mathcal{F}(f(t)) \\ &= e^{-j\omega \tau} F(\omega) \end{align*}$$ The symbols $t$ and $T$ are dummy variables of integration. As long as they represent the same domain of integration, i.e. the range over which the integration is performed (here, the entire real line $-\infty, \infty$), they do not affect the result of the integral. The outcome of an integral depends only on the function being integrated $f(.)$ and the limits of integration. If the domain of integration were different for the variables $t$ and $T$, then substituting one for the other might change the result.

The fourier transform of a shifted function is just the fourier transform of the original function multiplied by a constant related to the length of time of the shift $e^{-j\omega \tau}$.
